Dissertation Overview

Critical Making • GenAI • Game‑Based Rhet/Comp

This dissertation builds on my MFA project, where I explored generative AI's role in shaping teaching identity as a GTA. Moving beyond the use of AI for lesson planning and classroom management, my dissertation will examine how embedding generative AI into the creation of a pedagogical artifact — a game prototype for First-Year Composition (FYC) — reshapes learning, reflection, and pedagogy. The game will be built collaboratively using open-source AI tools for code, design, sound, and music.

The project addresses challenges of student disengagement and limited technical literacy among instructors by leveraging AI to close known technological literacy gaps while maintaining a reflective, problem-posing educational approach to game design. By enacting Freirean problem-posing pedagogy and critical digital literacy through gameplay, the prototype will examine authorship, creativity, and education in the AI age.

Guiding Research Questions

  • Authorship & Agency: What does the practice of critically making a GenAI-assisted game prototype reveal about authorship, agency, and knowledge acquisition?
  • Pedagogy & Mechanics: How can Freirean problem-posing and critical digital literacy be translated into game mechanics to scaffold FYC outcomes and reduce student apathy?
  • Faculty Development: How can critical making insights support faculty training in AI and digital literacy to help instructors adopt similar pedagogical practices?

This primary list establishes the theoretical landscape for analyzing generative AI and using a Digital Humanities methodology. I. Critical Making as Methodology

This group establishes "critical making" as the core methodology of my dissertation. Ratto's text defines the field, with Piaget and Papert's works as its pedagogical foundations. The other texts offer contemporary applications, linking the hands-on, critical ethos of making to the specific challenges and affordances of AI. They will inform a research-through-building method for my AI‑assisted game prototype.

II. Authorship, Copyright & Labor

These texts trace the concept of authorship from its legal and historical origins to the current state of flux in the digital age. Rose and Boyle provide the essential background on the invention of copyright and the public domain. Potts extends this inquiry to the internet age, setting the stage for a critical discussion of how generative AI further complicates traditional notions of creation, ownership, and intellectual property. Weeks and Srnicek offer frameworks for considering how AI might fundamentally alter the nature of labor and value, thus disrupting the value of intellectual property and labor.

V. Agency, Systems & Posthumanism

This final group on the primary list engages with the most speculative and philosophical questions raised by AI. Marcuse provides a classic critical theory lens on technology and society. Hayles’ work is central to posthumanist thought, questioning the boundaries of cognition and agency, while Harari and Kurzweil offer sweeping, forward-looking narratives about the co-evolution of humans and intelligent systems. Mitchell's work provides a clear-eyed guide to how AI actually functions, demystifying the technology for a non-expert audience. Li offers an insider's view from the perspective of a leading researcher who helped build these systems, while Buolamwini provides an external critique of the systemic biases and ethical failures embedded within them.

B. Game Design & Play

This section provides the theoretical and practical vocabulary for game design. Tekinbas and Zimmerman’s Rules of Play is a foundational, comprehensive text on game design fundamentals. Koster and Costikyan explore core experiential elements like fun and uncertainty, while Bogost and Flanagan articulate the concept of "procedural rhetoric" and "critical play," framing games as systems that make arguments and can be used for social critique. McGonigal and Salen explore the potential of games to effect real-world change and create learning ecologies. James Paul Gee’s work serves as a crucial bridge, explicitly connecting games to theories of literacy and learning. His texts argue that video games are powerful learning environments that embody modern pedagogical principles. They provide the central argument for why a game is a suitable medium for the pedagogical goals outlined in this project.
